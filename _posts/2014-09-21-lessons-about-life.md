My grandfather, Lyman, died ironically: in an attempt to keep his heart strong, his heart failed him; while running on a treadmill a heart attack produced his collapse, subsequent coma, and eventual death. I can’t help but to reminded of one of my other grandfather’s many sayings: “When I die and go to heaven God is going to tell me, ‘Salad is unhealthy; exercise is bad for you. You should have drank more beer.’”

I think the hardest part of being old enough to comprehend the death of your grandparents is that you are suddenly forced into the uncomfortable position of supporting your grieving parent. Uncomfortable![Papa and Granny M]({{ site.url }}/images/papagrannym.jpg){: .image-pull-right} not because it’s awkward that your parents are sad, but more that you have absolutely no idea what you’re supposed to do or say; that’s your parents’ job to know that stuff. Then you realize that their own parent, who’s supposed to be there to guide them on how to care for their own grieving children, is no longer able to. It’s a circle of Suck.

But I played my role, I think, decently enough. I don’t believe I cried at all during that long week, trying lend a sturdy arm to my mother and grandmother or to spend time with my little cousins, whom seemed quite distressed that all these adults were so sad - and yet instinctively understood that this was not the time to a pest, giving their parents some peace in a time that was anything but peaceful. Perhaps the greatest tragedy was not my grandfather’s actual death but this: that my cousins will never understand, truly, why all those adults were crying.

My desire to be helpful and strong for my family, admittedly, only partially caused my stoic demeanor. I was - am - wholly certain that he’s in a better place - a destination that he spent his whole life trying to comprehend.

A much younger version of myself was once sitting with Lyman on a swing that hangs from the ceiling of their second story porch at their home in Florida. I’m not quite sure what prompted this dialog - perhaps a childish inquisition? Or maybe an old man’s keen interest in the inner thoughts of a child? - but it remains my life’s most indelible lesson.

“What do you think about God and religion?”

“They seem silly to me, Papa. Each and every day scientists are uncovering new bits of knowledge, mapping the laws of the universe, solving mysteries. Soon we’ll be flying around in space! A thousand years ago, who would have ever thought that man would be able to travel to the stars? That was the realm of the gods, unknowable and unreachable - yet now it’s within our grasp!”

And his potent rejoinder: “But what of the mystery of Love? Surely, we can give names to and write books about all the chemicals and hormones in our body that together produce the flutter we feel in our hearts while under love’s spell. But why do we love? Science can never explain it; it is unknowable. And such is God.”

I had no reply. I could not deconstruct the mystery of Love. My favorite passage from my favorite book, The Boy Detective Fails, describes this moment of clarity better than I could ever hope to:

> "Have you ever been in love, sir?"
>
>
> "Oh, my poor, poor childish detective. Surely you must know by now. Love is the invention of man. It
> does not exist. It is a fairy tale designed to keep order. Imagine how we as humans would behave if
> we freed ourselves from the idiocy of that one particular idea: what a wonderful world; a world of
> absolute possibility."
>
>
> "I think I may be in love, sir."
>
>
> "May I ask how you know? How can you prove it? You are a detective, no? Where is the evidence? What
> clues are you basing this foolish assumption on?"
>
>
> "I don't know, really. It just occurs as a feeling in my hands and behind my knees."
>
>
> "But can it be placed in a bell jar? Can it be seen under a microscope? How can something as
> invisible - as insubstantial - as love ever hope to last?"
>
>
> "I cannot stop thinking about kissing her."
>
>
> "That is chemistry - or biology - it has nothing to do with hearts and flowers and the like. Do not
> be confused by what the natural world knows: We are all, in our own way, completely and totally
> alone. If love is real, it is a complete and total failing of the intellect. It is utter self-
> destruction. It is pandemonium."

But I cannot stop thinking about my grandfather. Whether aware of it or not, Lyman had set me on a course I believe he himself travelled: to try to understand the mystery of Love or maybe more accurately, marvel at it. And this unwitting gift he gave me has shaped me more as a person than anything else, from my personality to my conservative politics (Edmund Burke: “Politics ought to be adjusted not to human reasonings but to human nature, of which the reason is but a part, and by no means the greatest part.”). It has forced me to stay humble and live in the present - I don’t have all the answers and I never will (Shocking admission, I know). It has convinced me that I have a higher responsibility than just myself whether it be to Mankind, God, or Something. And so: the great tragedy is that my cousins were never given the opportunity to be lured down this marvelous path by this marvelous man. I can only hope that one day I will be able to pass on this curious joy that is Love, just as my Papa did for me.

I didn’t want to see Lyman as he laid upon a hospital bed, only a shell of the man he once was. I didn’t want to spoil my memories of him in life with those of him near death. It was a bit selfish, but I stayed at my grandparents house while the others went to the hospital. My mom told me of how when they read scripture to him it would elicit the greatest reactions from my braindead grandfather. She remarked, “It kind of makes you think that there might be something to this whole God thing.” My unspoken response: “I know there is.”
